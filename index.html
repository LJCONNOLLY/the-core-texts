// Overview

This reading list sits at the intersection of digital humanities, critical race and feminist theory, media and technology studies, and rhetoric/technical communication. Spanning roughly 1936–2024, it reveals a coherent scholarly project concerned above all with power as it operates through information systems, archives, interfaces, and designed objects — and with the communities who resist, reroute, or reimagine those systems.

// Key Disciplinary Questions Right Now

Derived by cross-referencing the 55-text candidacy list with the Potential Core List (30 texts). Where both lists independently return to the same authors and problems, that convergence signals what the field is genuinely asking right now.

Emerging signal — Ahmed, What's the Use?
A newer addition not in the core 55 but prominent in the potential list. Ahmed asks who gets to define what counts as "useful" — and for whom. This represents the field's push toward interrogating the foundational assumptions behind tool-building itself, not just critiquing existing tools. Watch for this to become a standard theoretical anchor alongside Noble and Eubanks.

// The List as an Argument

Read as a whole, this list makes a coherent argument: digital technologies do not exist outside of history, power, and embodiment, and the work of digital humanities must be accountable to the communities most affected by the systems it studies and builds.

It begins with foundational media theorists who established that meaning and representation are always political. It moves through postcolonial and feminist extensions, grounds those moves in empirical cases of algorithmic harm, and studies of Black, Indigenous, queer, and feminist digital practice as resistance. It culminates in questions of method and practice: how do we do digital humanities and design in ways accountable to justice?

The presence of Kuhn and Latour asks a meta-question: are we in a paradigm shift in how we understand technology and human life? The weight of evidence across these 55 texts suggests yes — and that equity, embodiment, and power are not add-ons to digital humanities but its constitutive concerns.
